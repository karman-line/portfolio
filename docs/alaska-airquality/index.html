In the most polluted county in the United States, not everyone wants to fix the problem.

By Karthika Namboothiri

Picturesque Fairbanks in interior Alaska is a popular northern light spotting destination, owing to its clear skies and distance from major water sources. The ‘Aurora season’ also coincides with the region’s worst air quality days, when hazy conditions spark government warnings and visibility turns abysmal.

Fairbanks, combined with adjacent North Star, tops the list of the U.S. Environment Protection Agency (EPA)’s most polluted counties in the United States. The local hospital even offers a ‘clean air respite room’ for overwhelmed residents during peak winters.

However, with no polluting factories or refineries in its vicinity, and a meagre population of less than a 100,000, the primary source of this pollution is the soot from wood-stoves that residents use to warm their homes. Wood stoves, which closely resemble an enclosed fireplace, incinerate dry log and release smoke from chimneys, accounting for 60% to 80% of the pollution in the county as per the EPA.
